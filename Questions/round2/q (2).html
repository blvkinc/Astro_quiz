Determine the change in luminosity of the Sun as it transitions from burning hydrogen to burning helium

To determine the change in luminosity of the Sun as it transitions from burning hydrogen to burning helium, we can use the Stefan-Boltzmann law, which relates the luminosity of a star to its surface temperature and radius. The Stefan-Boltzmann law is given by:

L = 4πR^2σT^4

Where:

L = Luminosity

R = Radius of the star

σ = Stefan-Boltzmann constant

T = Surface temperature of the star

We are given the initial values for the Sun's surface temperature (T1 = 5780 K) and radius (R1 = 6.96 × 10^8 m) when it is burning hydrogen. We need to calculate the final values for the Sun's surface temperature (T2 = 3160 K) and radius (R2 = 1.26 × 10^11 m) when it is burning helium.

First, let's calculate the initial luminosity (L1) of the Sun when burning hydrogen:

L1 = 4πR1^2σT1^4

Now, let's calculate the final luminosity (L2) of the Sun when burning helium:

L2 = 4πR2^2σT2^4

Finally, we can calculate the factor by which the luminosity of the Sun increases:

Factor = L2 / L1

Let's plug in the values and calculate:

L1 = 4π(6.96 × 10^8)^2σ(5780^4)

L2 = 4π(1.26 × 10^11)^2σ(3160^4)

Factor = L2 / L1

After performing the calculations, we find that the factor by which the luminosity of the Sun increases is approximately 2987. Therefore, the luminosity of the Sun will increase by a factor of about 3000 due to the changes in surface temperature and radius when it transitions to burning helium.
